# ODES
## Ordered Divergence in Expressive Space
*A First-Principles Field Theory of Sequential Collective Artifact Production*

> "The instruction never changed. Every person received the same rule." — Field notes, New York City, 2026

---

## Provenance

This framework was not designed in advance. It was derived backward from what a single afternoon in a New York City park produced — one large sheet of paper, one written instruction, strangers, colored markers, and time. That sequencing is stated here at the front because it matters epistemically. The experiment did not test the framework. The framework was extracted from the experiment.

One large white sheet. A poster reading ART ≠ IDEAS / TAKE SOME PAPER, rendered in layered, expressive hand-drawn letterforms — itself a work of art. Colored markers. One verbal instruction: *draw a line.*

Every person took a piece of paper. Not most — every one.

The marks began as near-vertical tally strokes. By the session's end the sheet contained vertical lines, diagonal slashes, cursive loops, S-curves, a teal filled organic shape, a lightning bolt, a detailed mandala flower, a smiley face, a coiled spiral, and the written sentence: *Art is inextricable from ideas.*

The verbal instruction never changed. The behavior diverged completely — not randomly, but in a lawful, escalating, irreversible sequence that terminated only because the physical surface ran out of space.

No laboratory controls. No randomization. No sample size recorded. One session, one location, one afternoon. The claim is that what happened has a single structural explanation, and that explanation is general. That claim requires replication before it can be stated with confidence beyond a working hypothesis. The limitations listed at the end of this document define exactly what must be tested next.

## Three Anomalies

Three observations from the session require explanation:

- Why did complexity escalate monotonically rather than scattering around some mean?
- Why did escalation proceed in a lawful sequence rather than randomly across available forms?
- Why did one behavior — taking paper — remain perfectly invariant while the adjacent behavior — making a mark — departed entirely from its stated instruction?

These three anomalies have a single structural explanation. Every claim in this framework is a consequence of that explanation. The explanation is geometric.

## The Central Empirical Fact: The Written/Verbal Split

Two instructions governed the session. One was verbal, delivered by the experimenter at the moment of engagement: *draw a line.* One was written on the founding poster, present throughout the entire session: *take some paper.*

Two behaviors. Two compliance rates. One structural variable separating them.

**Taking paper:** compliance approximately 100%. Not approximately — every participant.

**Drawing a line:** compliance approximately 100% in the sense that everyone drew *something.* But the content of what was drawn departed entirely from the instruction. The behavior was satisfied; the mark's content was governed by everything except the instruction.

These are not the same phenomenon producing the same number. They are different mechanisms arriving at similar surface rates for fundamentally different reasons.

The written instruction — *take some paper* — was present on the poster throughout the entire session. It required no memory maintenance. It bore no social relationship to the experimenter. It was re-readable, permanent, and source-independent. Every participant encountered it equally, whether the experimenter was nearby or not. Compliance did not drift. The written instruction governed a behavior; that behavior was invariant.

The verbal instruction — *draw a line* — achieved compliance through active social engagement: an experimenter present, invitation clear, context rich. But as the accumulated surface grew more complex, finite agent attention shifted from the verbal instruction (held in working memory, competing for cognitive resources) to the sheet (visually present, growing in information content with each mark). The verbal instruction was outcompeted.

This split is the founding empirical fact of the framework. Everything else is its elaboration.

## Part I — What Kind of System This Is

### 1.1 The Failure of Equilibrium Models

Any attempt to explain the park sheet using equilibrium assumptions fails on first contact with the data.

An equilibrium account would predict that participants, receiving the same instruction, produce a distribution of marks centered on some modal interpretation of "a line." Variance would average out. Deviations from the mean would diminish as the session progressed. More participants would mean more convergence, not more deviation. The system would be ergodic: path-independent, history-forgetting, convergent.

The data show the structural opposite on every count.

Complexity does not converge. It escalates monotonically. History is not forgotten — it is permanently encoded in the accumulated surface, and every subsequent mark is conditioned on that accumulated history. A different sequence of agents, starting from a different initial mark, would produce a different trajectory through the space of possible outputs, and the two trajectories would diverge with each subsequent participant.

The equilibrium account is not approximately right. It predicts the wrong sign on every observable.

### 1.2 The System as an Open, Irreversible Structure

The park sheet is an open system. Agents continuously enter from the environment, import cognitive energy in the form of attention, intention, and identity-expression drive, and leave behind an irreversible structural deposit: a committed mark. The sheet is not isolated. Each new participant is an energy input; each committed mark permanently alters the landscape for everyone who follows.

The four structural properties of this system:

**Openness.** The system exchanges cognitive energy with its environment at every moment a new participant approaches.

**Far-from-equilibrium driving.** The distinctiveness pressure on each agent — the pressure to produce a mark perceptually distinct from all prior marks — increases monotonically as the session progresses.

**Nonlinear feedback.** Each mark reduces the available distinctiveness in its neighborhood of the expressive space, increasing the pressure on subsequent agents to travel further. The feedback is autocatalytic: marks generate more-complex marks.

**Irreversibility.** No mark can be undone. The system evolves in one direction only.

The order produced by the park sheet — the sequential emergence of increasingly complex representational forms, transitioning across categorical registers in a specific, lawful sequence — is a consequence of these four properties interacting. It is order from irreversibility. The parallels to dissipative structures in the thermodynamic sense (Prigogine 1977) are structural and deliberate, and are examined in Part XI. The primary claim is not borrowed from thermodynamics; it is derived from the geometry of the expressive field.

### 1.3 The Arrow of Time

In the park sheet, the arrow of time is strong: not only does informational complexity increase monotonically, but the specific path of increase is unrepeatable. Each session is a unique trajectory through the expressive phase space. There is no return path and no repeat path.

This is the first and most fundamental claim of the ODES framework: **the system has a permanent, path-dependent arrow of time.** Everything else follows from this.

## Part II — The Expressive Field: A Riemannian Manifold

### 2.1 Definition

The Expressive Field **E** is the space of all possible marks on the shared surface. It is a Riemannian manifold parameterized by six independent coordinates:

| Parameter | Symbol | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Topology (register) | τ | Representational mode: line · curve · loop · closed area · figure · glyph · text |
| Scale | λ | Spatial extent, length, area covered |
| Orientation | θ | Angle relative to surface axes |
| Stroke weight | w | Thickness, density, pressure |
| Chromatic position | c | Hue, saturation, luminance value |
| Surface location | (x, y) | Position on the shared surface |

A mark is a point **q** = (τ, λ, θ, w, c, x, y) ∈ **E**. The space of all possible marks is the full manifold **E**.

### 2.2 The Perceptual Metric

**E** is not Euclidean. The correct metric on **E** is not the abstract coordinate difference between two marks but the perceptual distance — how different the marks appear to the visual system of a human observer. This metric is Riemannian: locally Euclidean but globally curved, where the curvature encodes the non-uniformities of human visual sensitivity.

Several such non-uniformities are well-documented:

**Cardinal orientation bias.** The visual system is disproportionately sensitive to deviations from vertical and horizontal. A 5° rotation near vertical is more perceptually salient than the same rotation near 45°. The metric on the θ-axis has wells at θ = 0° and θ = 90°.

**Scale compression.** Small marks near the limit of visual resolution are perceptually closer together than large marks spanning the visual field. The metric on the λ-axis is logarithmic at large scales.

**Chromatic non-uniformity.** Perceptual color space is not linear in CIE coordinates. The metric on the c-axis reflects the empirically measured non-uniformities of human color discrimination.

**Topological discontinuity.** Most critically: the τ-axis is not continuous. It is a discrete set of representational categories with genuine topological barriers between them. You cannot smoothly vary from "line" to "filled area" — these are categorically distinct modes separated by a discontinuity that has no continuous intermediate path.

Formally: for all parameters except τ, the metric tensor g_**E**(q) is smooth and positive-definite. For τ, the distance function between adjacent registers d(τᵢ, τᵢ₊₁) has a topological gap — a finite minimum cost that cannot be reduced by any intermediate path.

The metric g_**E** has not yet been empirically measured across all six parameter axes. This measurement is possible using established psychophysical methods (discrimination tasks across the parameter axes) and is a required step before quantitative predictions from the framework can be tested. See Part XII.

### 2.3 The Register as the Critical Parameter

The register **τ** is the single most important parameter in **E**. It alone is topologically discontinuous: it does not admit smooth interpolation between values. This is what produces the phase transitions observed in the data.

All other parameters can be varied continuously. An agent can produce a mark that is slightly larger, slightly more tilted, slightly thicker, or slightly redder than any prior mark. The register parameter does not admit this. There is no continuous path from "line" to "filled organic shape."

This crossing has a measurable cost. Crossing from one register to an adjacent register requires higher cognitive commitment, higher expressive bandwidth, and — in public settings — higher social exposure cost. The crossing cost C(τᵢ → τᵢ₊₁) is finite and positive for every register transition. It varies across transitions; crossing from lines to curves is cheaper than crossing from figurative marks to propositional text. But no transition is free.

The crossing costs define a ridge structure on the register axis: ridges of finite height separating each register from its neighbors, with the ridges growing higher as the registers grow more topologically distant from the initial instruction.

## Part III — The Pressure Landscape

### 3.1 Definition

At any moment t during the session, the accumulated state of the sheet **S**ₜ — the set of all marks made by participants 1 through t — defines a Pressure Landscape over **E**:

```
P(q | Sₜ)  ∝  D_g(q, Sₜ)
```

where D_g(q, **S**ₜ) is the geodesic distance from the proposed mark **q** to the nearest existing mark in **S**ₜ, measured under the Riemannian metric g_**E**.

High pressure means the region is unoccupied and available. Low pressure means the region is already claimed. Agents driven by identity-expression needs are attracted toward high-pressure regions — toward territory in **E** not yet claimed by prior participants.

### 3.2 The Three Structural Laws

**Law 1 — The Ratchet.** Every committed mark permanently lowers the pressure P in its perceptual neighborhood. Pressure at any point in **E** can only decrease or remain constant — it cannot increase. There is no restoring force. Formally:

```
For all q ∈ E and all t' > t:  P(q | Sₜ') ≤ P(q | Sₜ)
```

The ratchet is strict. The system evolves in one direction. This is the direct structural analog of the second law of thermodynamics in the expressive domain, but the claim is geometric, not borrowed from thermodynamics.

**Law 2 — Geometric Escalation.** As occupied regions expand, maintaining equivalent distinctiveness requires traveling further in **E**. The geodesic distance sufficient for distinctiveness at session-time t₃ is insufficient at session-time t₁₃. This is not a cultural choice or a creative ambition. It is arithmetic: a space is filling, and reaching the same distance from the nearest occupied point requires going further as the space fills. Complexity escalation is the inevitable geometry of a filling Riemannian manifold.

**Law 3 — Ridge Structure.** The register transitions τᵢ → τᵢ₊₁ are topological ridges in the Pressure Landscape — barriers of finite height that are costly to cross. An agent crosses a ridge only when:

```
P_within ≤ C_ridge
```

i.e., when the pressure available within the current register falls below the cost of crossing to the adjacent register. Below this threshold, the gradient of available pressure points across the ridge rather than along the current register. The agent crosses not in defiance of the geometry but in obedience to it.

### 3.3 Informational Entropy

Define the informational entropy of the accumulated state **S**ₜ as the Shannon entropy of the mark distribution across **E**:

```
H(Sₜ)  =  −Σ_q  p(q | Sₜ) · log p(q | Sₜ)
```

where p(q | **S**ₜ) is the empirical density of marks in the neighborhood of **q**. This quantity has three properties that follow directly from the three laws:

**Monotonic increase.** Each new mark occupies previously unoccupied territory. H(**S**ₜ) is non-decreasing for all t.

**Discontinuous jumps at register transitions.** When the session crosses a register boundary — the first filled shape, the first figurative mark, the first propositional text — H(**S**ₜ) jumps discontinuously. A new topological region of **E** is entered, expanding the covered space qualitatively rather than quantitatively. These are the bifurcation events.

**Path dependence.** H(**S**ₜ) encodes the full history of the session's trajectory through **E**. Two sessions with different initial conditions produce different entropy trajectories that diverge irreversibly with each subsequent participant.

The entropy production rate σ(t) = dH/dt is moderate in the interior of a register, elevated at register transitions, and maximal when propositional text appears — because the entire linguistic register of **E**, vast and previously unexplored, is entered for the first time.

## Part IV — The Attention Allocation Theorem

### 4.1 The Two-Signal Problem

Every participant faced two simultaneous signals before marking:

**Signal A — the instruction:** *Draw a line.* Fixed. Unambiguous. Identical for every agent throughout the entire session. Information load I_A is constant.

**Signal B — the accumulated surface:** The growing, increasingly complex collection of all prior marks. Not fixed. Not unambiguous. Unique at every moment t. Information load I_B(t) is strictly increasing.

Signal A costs near-zero attention to process at session onset: it is short, verbal, explicit, and unambiguous. Signal B costs attention proportional to the complexity of the accumulated state **S**ₜ: a sparse sheet is cheap to read; a dense, complex sheet demands sustained visual attention to parse.

### 4.2 The Theorem

**[ODES-T1] Attention Reallocation under Growing Complexity.** Let κ be an agent's finite attention bandwidth. Then the attention allocated to Signal A decreases monotonically with session time t:

```
α_A(t)  =  κ · I_A / (I_A + I_B(t))

dα_A/dt  <  0    (strictly, since I_B(t) is strictly increasing)
```

At session onset (t small, **S**ₜ sparse): I_B ≈ 0, α_A ≈ 1. The instruction dominates. Early agents draw something close to a line.

At late session (t large, **S**ₜ complex): I_B >> I_A, α_A → 0. The accumulated surface dominates. The instruction is not forgotten — it is outcompeted for cognitive resources by the growing visual complexity of the sheet.

This theorem is derived analytically from the assumption of finite attention bandwidth and strictly increasing I_B(t). Its prediction — that late-session marks should diverge further from the instruction than early-session marks, and that this divergence should correlate with session complexity — is empirically testable and has not yet been tested under controlled conditions.

Rule drift does not require disobedience or creativity. Late-session participants allocate their finite cognitive resources to the signal that is most demanding and most informative for the task of finding distinctiveness. This is rational attention allocation, not norm violation.

### 4.3 The Instruction Paradox

**[ODES-H1]** *The richer the instruction, the faster it is abandoned.* A more elaborate instruction carries a higher initial I_A, but this makes it outcompeted by I_B(t) faster, not slower, because I_B(t) needs to grow less before it exceeds a higher threshold.

This is a working hypothesis derived analytically from ODES-T1. It is not supported by the founding experiment, which used only one instruction format. It is stated here as a conjecture requiring experimental test: two sessions, one with an elaborate instruction and one with a minimal instruction, run with equivalent participant pools and equivalent founding prompts.

The only instruction that could survive indefinitely is one that scales with I_B(t) — one that updates to respond to the accumulated state. A fixed instruction cannot compete with a growing field.

## Part V — Agent Trajectory Mechanics

Every participant navigates the Pressure Landscape through four sequential acts. These are the necessary structure of any agent with finite cognitive resources navigating a curved potential field under a growing information load.

**Act I — Field Reading.** The agent scans the accumulated surface, constructing a local map of the Pressure Landscape: which regions of **E** are saturated, where the pressure gradient points, what territory remains open. Map precision scales with available attention bandwidth κ. A glance produces a coarse map — the agent moves toward the most obvious unoccupied territory. Sustained examination produces a fine map — the agent identifies subtle gaps invisible to a casual scan.

Coarse maps produce gradient-following along the steepest locally visible path. Fine maps permit identification of genuinely underexplored pockets. This is why the mandala and the terminal sentence required more time. They required finer maps.

**Act II — Gradient Following.** The agent moves through **E** along the path of minimum resistance toward maximum distinctiveness from all prior marks. Their starting position in this movement is shaped by personal history and cultural toolkit. But the direction of movement is governed by the Pressure Landscape, not by personal preference in isolation.

This is not origination of something from nothing. It is navigation of a field. The agent moves in the direction of steepest available distinctiveness, not in the direction they arrived planning to go.

**Act III — Natural Stopping.** The agent stops when one of three conditions is met:

- **(a) Distinctiveness achieved:** The proposed mark has sufficient geodesic distance from all prior marks in g_**E**. The agent has reached territory genuinely their own.
- **(b) Bandwidth exhausted:** The agent has committed the cognitive resources they brought.
- **(c) Gradient reversal:** Further movement would reduce distinctiveness — the gradient of available pressure reverses as the agent approaches territory already occupied from the other side.

Condition (c) explains why the mandala did not fill the entire sheet. The agent followed the pressure gradient until further elaboration would have started reducing perceptual distinctiveness from existing marks — until elaboration began to loop back toward occupied territory rather than away from it. Then they stopped. This is the natural stopping condition of gradient-following in a bounded potential field.

**Act IV — Irreversible Commitment.** The mark is drawn. Ink touches paper. This single act permanently alters the Pressure Landscape for every subsequent participant. There is no undo. The entropy H(**S**ₜ) increases. The system's trajectory through **E** is extended by one irreversible step.

Irreversibility is not a limitation of the medium. It is the generative mechanism of the framework. Without irreversibility, there is no ratchet. Without a ratchet, there is no monotonic pressure escalation. Without escalation, there are no register crossings. Without register crossings, there is no ordered complexity sequence. The art is a consequence of the permanence of the marks.

## Part VI — Register Topology: Phase Transitions in the Expressive Field

### 6.1 The Register Sequence as a Topological Necessity

As the session progresses and within-register pressure falls below the crossing cost C_ridge, agents cross into adjacent registers. This is **Register Escape**: not rebellion, not autonomous creativity, but the gradient pointing across the topological ridge because within-register territory is exhausted.

The register escape sequence observed in the founding session:

```
τ₀  Vertical strokes
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₁  Diagonal strokes, varied angles
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₂  Cursive loops, S-curves, figure-eights
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₃  Filled organic shape (2-dimensional area)
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₄  Figurative marks (lightning bolt, smiley face)
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₅  Complex figurative (mandala, spiral)
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₆  Propositional text
```

This sequence is not arbitrary. It follows the topological structure of **E**: registers are ordered by increasing topological complexity, increasing cognitive cost, and increasing informational density. The sequence from τ₀ to τ₆ is the sequence of increasing topological dimension in representational space.

This is a conjecture, not an established fact from a single session: alternative orderings of this sequence should be impossible or very rare in sessions with equivalent initial conditions. The sequence is determined by the topology of **E**, not by the cultural preferences of participants. This claim requires replication to assess.

### 6.2 The Terminal Bifurcation

The written sentence — *Art is inextricable from ideas* — is the terminal and most dramatic register escape. By the time that agent arrived, the visual registers τ₀ through τ₅ were substantially occupied. Every topological category of visual mark was represented. The one region of **E** with near-zero prior occupancy was propositional linguistic space.

No prior participant had placed a connected propositional claim on the sheet. The pressure in the linguistic register was absolute — the most distant unoccupied region accessible to a human agent. An agent with sufficient bandwidth, intellectual engagement with the founding premise, and willingness to pay the highest crossing cost in the session followed the gradient all the way to the edge of the manifold and placed a counter-proposition.

*Art is inextricable from ideas* directly contradicts the poster's claim ART ≠ IDEAS. This is not coincidence. It is the geometry of the Pressure Landscape operating on a system whose founding premise was itself a debatable proposition: the most occupied region of **E** was the visual-expressive register established by the poster; the most available territory was the propositional-argumentative register — the one in which the poster had made a claim but no participant had yet responded in kind.

The poster said: *Art does not equal ideas.* The field said: *Art is inextricable from ideas.* The instruction generated the conditions. The field generated the argument.

### 6.3 Phase Transition Geometry

Each register escape is a bifurcation. The prior organized state — the current register, with its coherent cluster of marks and diminishing available pressure — becomes insufficient. A new organized state — the next register, with its fresh territory and high available pressure — becomes accessible. The system leaps discontinuously to the new state.

Define the **Register Bifurcation Condition:**

```
P_within(t)  ≤  C(τ_current → τ_next)
```

When this condition is crossed, an agent with sufficient expressive bandwidth crosses the register ridge. The sequence of register escapes in the session is the sequence of these bifurcations. The bifurcation parameter is the within-register pressure P_within relative to the crossing cost C_ridge — and the crossing cost is endogenously generated by the agents' own prior marks. The system drives itself toward its own bifurcation points.

## Part VII — The Invariant: A Bandwidth Budget Theorem

### 7.1 The Two Behaviors

The founding session produced one perfect behavioral invariant: everyone took a piece of paper. Not most — everyone. In a session where the expressive behavior departed so completely from its stated instruction that it ended in propositional philosophical argument, the transactional behavior of paper-taking never deviated.

This is not a coincidence. It is a theorem — one that follows analytically from ODES-T1 and requires empirical verification across replications.

### 7.2 The Theorem

**[ODES-T2] The Invariant.** Let B_A be a behavior that requires near-zero cognitive bandwidth to execute — it is motoric, unambiguous, and socially visible as a compliance signal. Let B_B be a behavior that requires navigating the Pressure Landscape and therefore competes for the same finite bandwidth as field-reading and gradient-following.

As I_B(t) grows with session time t:

```
B_A compliance         →  1   (invariant)
B_B instruction-following  →  0   (total drift)
```

The argument: Paper-taking satisfies the bandwidth conditions for B_A. It is motoric (pick up paper), unambiguous (the paper is there, the motion is clear), and socially visible (other participants can observe compliance). Its information cost I_{B_A} is near-zero and does not grow with session complexity. Therefore the bandwidth available for paper-taking is not affected by the growth of I_B(t) — the two tasks do not compete for the same cognitive resource. Paper-taking is shielded from the attention reallocation mechanism that governs mark-making.

Mark-making fails this condition because it competes directly for the same cognitive bandwidth as Pressure Landscape navigation. The more attention the landscape demands, the less is available for instruction-following. B_B rule-following falls monotonically as I_B(t) grows.

The invariant and the drift are not two separate phenomena. They are the same phenomenon — finite attention allocation — observed from two sides simultaneously.

### 7.3 Identifying Invariant Behaviors a Priori

The Invariant Theorem yields a practical prediction for any setting in which sequential agents comply with an instruction while making an expressive decision: apply two tests to each element of the instruction. Does it require near-zero cognitive bandwidth? Does it carry social compliance visibility? Any element passing both tests will be invariant regardless of how far the adjacent expressive behaviors drift. Any element failing either test is subject to drift proportional to I_B(t).

This prediction has not yet been tested beyond the founding session.

## Part VIII — Anti-Ergodic Accumulation

### 8.1 Definition

A system is ergodic if, given sufficient time, it explores its state space uniformly and settles into a stationary distribution. Ergodic systems are path-independent, history-forgetting, and convergent. Their time averages equal ensemble averages. Deviations from the mean are temporary and average out.

The park sheet is structurally incapable of any of these properties.

**[ODES-T3] Anti-Ergodic Accumulation.** Any system satisfying the four ODES conditions — (1) agents have identity-expression drives, (2) the surface is publicly observable, (3) outputs are irreversible, (4) no coordination payoff exists for similarity — cannot converge to any stationary distribution.

The argument for non-convergence: Convergence to a stationary distribution π on **E** would require that agents produce marks in any region R ⊂ **E** at a stable rate. But the Ratchet Law guarantees that each mark in R permanently reduces the attractiveness of R for subsequent agents: P(q | **S**ₜ) for q ∈ R decreases monotonically. No stable rate of production in R is possible. The distribution over marks cannot stabilize — it must shift toward unoccupied territory permanently.

The argument that detailed balance fails: Standard ergodic systems satisfy detailed balance: π(A)T(A→B) = π(B)T(B→A). In the park system, irreversibility makes the reverse transition impossible. The mark made by agent 3 cannot be unmade by agent 7. T(B→A) = 0 for all A ≠ B. Detailed balance fails everywhere. The system is not merely non-ergodic — it violates the preconditions of ergodicity at the most fundamental level.

### 8.2 Path Dependence as a Structural Property

The accumulated state **S**ₜ at time t is the complete, indelible history of the session from t=0 to t. Every trajectory decision made by every prior agent is permanently encoded in the Pressure Landscape that the current agent navigates.

Strong empirical corollary: two sessions with identical instructions and equivalent participant populations, seeded with different initial marks, will produce statistically distinguishable trajectories with probability approaching 1 as the number of participants grows. The initial seed determines the curvature of the entire subsequent trajectory. Small differences in initial conditions compound with each participant.

The system did not terminate by reaching equilibrium. It terminated because the physical surface ran out of space. The theoretical field **E** is unbounded. The paper is not.

## Part IX — The Poster as Prior: The Founder Effect

### 9.1 The Prompt Is Already in the Field

The participants encountered the instruction not in a vacuum but on a poster rendered in multiple colors, large irregular hand-drawn letterforms, layered stylized strokes with evident aesthetic care. The ≠ symbol itself was expressive. The poster was art. It was art making a claim about art.

Every participant encountered this visual object before approaching the mark sheet. It instantiated an initial Pressure Landscape **S**₀ before the first mark was drawn. The tally strokes of the earliest participants are not simple relative to zero complexity — they are simple relative to the poster's complexity. The first agents were already navigating a field shaped by a prior aesthetic commitment.

The experiment did not begin when the first participant marked. It began when the poster was made.

### 9.2 The Founder Effect

Define the **Founder Effect** as the initial curvature of the Pressure Landscape set by the founding prompt before any participant marks. This curvature is the initial condition of the system. It determines:

- Which regions of **E** are already occupied or perceptually depleted at the session's start
- Which regions therefore carry the highest initial pressure
- The direction of the first participants' gradient-following
- Consequently, the entire subsequent bifurcation sequence

The poster establishes a pressure well in the visual-aesthetic register. It occupies **E** in the direction of expressive hand-drawn visual complexity. The first participants cannot achieve distinctiveness in that direction — it is already claimed by the poster. They go to the simplest available territory: near-vertical strokes that are maximally distant from the poster's complexity while still being marks. The simplicity of the first marks is not simplicity relative to zero. It is simplicity relative to the founding aesthetic.

**[ODES-T4] Founder Effect on Bifurcation Timing.** Let P₀ have informational entropy H₀ > 0 in **E**. Then the first register escape will occur after fewer participants than in a session seeded by a null prompt (H₀ = 0), because the initial Pressure Landscape already has partial occupancy that compresses the within-register availability for the first participants.

The founding prompt is the session's first participant — the one agent whose mark persists throughout the entire session and who never returns to encounter the field they left behind.

### 9.3 The Founding Proposition

The poster did not only set a visual prior. ART ≠ IDEAS is a debatable claim. It invited a response. The terminal written sentence — *Art is inextricable from ideas* — is both the highest-entropy event in the expressive sequence and a direct reply to the founding proposition. Sessions without a propositional founding premise are less likely to produce propositional terminal events. The content of the terminal event is shaped by the content of the founding mark.

**[ODES-H2]** *Propositional founding premises produce propositional terminal events.* This is a working hypothesis. It requires experimental test: sessions with a debatable founding claim compared against sessions with a neutral founding artifact, controlling for visual complexity.

## Part X — The ODES Equations

### 10.1 The State Equation

The state of the system at time t is fully specified by **S**ₜ. The evolution equation is:

```
S_{t+1}  =  Sₜ ∪ {q*(t+1)}

q*(t+1)  =  argmax_{q ∈ E \ Sₜ}  P(q | Sₜ)    subject to  ‖q‖_κ ≤ κ_{t+1}
```

where P(q | **S**ₜ) = D_g(q, **S**ₜ) is the pressure at q given accumulated state **S**ₜ, and ‖q‖_κ is the cognitive cost of producing mark q, bounded by agent t+1's attention bandwidth κ_{t+1}.

Each agent marks the point in **E** with the highest available distinctiveness pressure that they can reach within their attention budget. This is the ODES trajectory equation.

### 10.2 The Entropy Production Equation

The informational entropy production at each step:

```
ΔH(t)  =  H(S_{t+1}) − H(Sₜ)  =  D_g(q*(t+1), Sₜ) · ρ(q*(t+1))
```

where ρ(q) is the local density of unexplored territory in the neighborhood of q in **E**. At register transitions, ρ jumps discontinuously — a new topological region opens. These are the entropy production peaks at bifurcation events.

### 10.3 The Register Escape Condition

Register escape from τ_current to τ_next occurs when:

```
P_max(τ_current | Sₜ)  ≤  C(τ_current → τ_next)
```

where P_max(τ_current | **S**ₜ) is the maximum available pressure within the current register, and C(τ_current → τ_next) is the crossing cost of the topological ridge between them. At this moment, the gradient points across the ridge. An agent with sufficient bandwidth crosses.

## Part XI — Connections to Existing Work

These are structural parallels, not citations deployed for authority. Each confirms a component of the ODES mechanism or reveals precisely where the framework differs from prior accounts.

**Dissipative Structures (Prigogine 1977).** Open systems far from thermodynamic equilibrium self-organize into ordered states sustained by irreversible energy flow, undergoing discontinuous bifurcations as the system is driven beyond successive instability thresholds. The park sheet exhibits all four structural conditions of a dissipative system: openness, far-from-equilibrium driving, nonlinear feedback, irreversibility. Each register escape has the structure of a Hopf bifurcation: the prior organized state loses stability, and the system transitions discontinuously to a new, more complex organized state. The critical difference: in chemical dissipative structures, the driving parameter is externally controlled. In the expressive system, the driving parameter — within-register pressure — is endogenously generated by the agents' own prior marks. The system drives itself toward its bifurcation points.

**Iterated Learning (Kirby, Cornish & Smith 2008).** Artificial languages transmitted across participant chains became more structured through compression pressure — agents simplified and regularized to ease transmission. The ODES framework is the structural inverse: same iterated transmission architecture, divergence pressure instead of compression pressure. The direction of the gradient fully determines whether the system converges or diverges. Both results follow from the same underlying mechanism — agents modify outputs relative to prior outputs — applied to opposite gradient fields.

**Cumulative Cultural Ratchet (Caldwell & Millen 2008).** Paper plane designs improved across chains without explicit instruction through irreversible ratcheting. This ratchet mechanism is structurally identical to ODES's Ratchet Law. The difference: their ratchet improves along a functional performance dimension; ODES escalates along a complexity-in-**E** dimension. Both demonstrate that irreversibility + gradient = monotonic directed change, without requiring intent or coordination.

**Schema and Correction (Gombrich 1960).** All representation begins with a prior schema corrected toward observation. In the ODES framework, the schema is the accumulated prior state **S**ₜ and the correction is driven by distinctiveness pressure rather than perceptual accuracy. Gombrich's mechanism correctly predicts that each correction is relative to what already exists — the gradient-following property. His corrections converge (toward accurate representation); ODES's corrections diverge (toward maximum distinctiveness from prior marks).

**Optimal Complexity (Berlyne 1971).** Humans prefer intermediate complexity — maximum engagement falls on the ascending edge of the inverted-U arousal function. The ODES framework predicts that each agent targets this ascending edge. As the sheet grows richer, the target moves upward. Complexity escalation tracks a moving optimality target defined by the accumulated state.

**Schelling Dynamics (Schelling 1978).** Minimal local rules produce dramatic macro-level patterns with no central coordination. The ODES framework is a Schelling-type system: the local rule is minimal ("be distinct from what is already there"), and the macro pattern emerges from purely local agent behavior. No coordination is needed. No intention toward complexity is needed. The complexity is produced by the geometry.

## Part XII — Where the Framework Is Incomplete

**Individual bandwidth is a free parameter.** ODES predicts that agents with higher attention bandwidth will travel further in **E** and cross register ridges more readily. It does not derive the distribution of bandwidth across agents from first principles. Why the mandala-maker and the text-writer had the bandwidth required for their register crossings — rather than merely predicting that some agent eventually would — is outside the framework's current scope.

**Crossing costs are unspecified.** The framework predicts register escapes when within-register pressure falls below the crossing cost C_ridge. It does not specify how C_ridge is determined. Is it purely cognitive (the topological distance in **E**)? Does it include social exposure cost (public visibility of norm departure)? Both factors are plausible. Different specifications produce different quantitative predictions about the timing of register escapes. A full specification would allow prediction of which register a session of N participants reaches, not just the qualitative sequence.

**N was not recorded.** The participant count in the founding session is unknown. The framework's predictions about when register escapes occur are functions of t. Without the actual sequence, these predictions cannot be tested against the existing data. Any replication must record participant count and sequence.

**The metric g_E is not yet empirically measured.** The framework requires the Riemannian metric of **E** to be specified by human perceptual sensitivity across all six parameter axes. This is measurable using established psychophysical methods but has not yet been done for the full manifold.

**One session, one city, one afternoon.** The framework claims generalizability across contexts. That claim requires replication in different physical settings, different cultural contexts, different instruction regimes, and different prompt aesthetics before it can be stated with confidence beyond a working hypothesis.

## Part XIII — Predictions for Replication

If the framework is correct, the following should hold in controlled replications:

**1. Register sequence is topologically necessary.** The ordering τ₀ → ... → τ₆ should replicate across independent sessions with equivalent initial conditions. Departures should be traceable to outlier-bandwidth agents, unusual initial conditions, or different poster aesthetics — not to random variation.

**2. Spatial clustering by register is measurable.** Marks should not be uniformly mixed across the sheet. They should cluster spatially because agents condition on locally visible prior marks rather than scanning the full sheet before marking. The spatial autocorrelation coefficient of mark complexity should be significantly positive. Null model: uniform spatial distribution.

**3. The invariant holds under extreme expressive drift.** Paper-taking compliance should remain near 100% even when the expressive behavior has fully departed from the verbal instruction. Test: track paper-taking compliance against mark register at late-session marks.

**4. Path dependence produces divergent trajectories.** Seed two sessions with different experimenter-planted initial marks. Run with equivalent participant pools. The trajectories through **E** should diverge, and divergence should increase with each subsequent participant. Measure divergence by comparing H(**S**ₜᴬ, **S**ₜᴮ) — the cross-entropy between the two sessions' accumulated states.

**5. Founder effect shifts bifurcation timing.** Two sessions: one with the aesthetically complex poster (H₀ > 0), one with a minimal text-only instruction (H₀ ≈ 0). The complex-poster session should produce earlier first register escape and reach higher final register depth at equivalent N.

**6. Entropy production accelerates at bifurcations.** If marks are coded by register and perceptual distance from prior marks, H(**S**ₜ) should show discontinuous positive jumps at register escape events, with the jump magnitude increasing with the register transition index.

**7. Propositional founding premises produce propositional terminal events.** Sessions with a debatable founding claim should produce written propositional responses at significantly higher rates than sessions with neutral founding artifacts, controlling for session length and participant count.

## Part XVI — The Claim

The same verbal instruction given to a sequence of social agents sharing an observable irreversible surface will always produce escalating expressive complexity — not sometimes, always — provided:

- Agents have any degree of identity-expression drive
- The surface is publicly observable
- Outputs are irreversible
- No coordination payoff exists for similarity

The complexity escalates in a lawful sequence determined by the topology of **E** and the Riemannian geometry of the Pressure Landscape. The sequence is not random. The bifurcations are predictable. The invariant is exactly explained. The anti-ergodic trajectory is structurally necessary.

This is not an aesthetic claim. It is a geometric one. The park sheet is an instance of a dynamical system. The same mechanism — irreversible outputs, distinctiveness pressure, finite bandwidth — operates at every scale where the four conditions hold: individual creative sessions, artistic traditions, collaborative online platforms, the evolution of language, the branching of cultural forms.

The instruction does not generate the art. The instruction generates the conditions. The conditions generate the pressure landscape. The pressure landscape generates the agent trajectories. The agent trajectories, through their irreversible committed marks, generate the landscape that generates the next trajectories.

The art is the history of the system's path through **E**. And the path through **E** is ordered, directed, and unrepeatable — because time, in this dynamics, runs in one direction.

The ink that cannot be erased is not the limitation of the medium. It is the arrow of time in the expressive field. Every mark that cannot be undone is an increment of pressure that drives the system further from equilibrium and closer to the next bifurcation. The mandala and the written sentence were not choices made by creative individuals.
