## Provenance

This framework was not designed in advance. It was derived backward from what a single afternoon in a New York City park produced — one large sheet of paper, one written instruction, strangers, colored markers, and time. That sequencing is stated here at the front because it matters epistemically. The experiment did not test the framework. The framework was extracted from the experiment.

One large white sheet. A poster reading **ART ≠ IDEAS / TAKE SOME PAPER** rendered in layered, expressive hand-drawn letterforms — itself a work of art. Colored markers. One instruction: *draw a line*.

Every person took a piece of paper. Not most — every one.

The marks began as near-vertical tally strokes. By the session's end the sheet contained vertical lines, diagonal slashes, cursive loops, a teal filled organic shape, S-curves, a lightning bolt, a detailed mandala flower, a smiley face, a coiled spiral, and the written sentence: *Art is inextricable from ideas*.

The instruction never changed. The behavior diverged completely — not randomly, but in a lawful, escalating, irreversible sequence that terminated only because the physical surface ran out of space.

**Three anomalies require explanation:**

1. Why did complexity escalate **monotonically** rather than scattering around some mean?
2. Why did escalation proceed in a **lawful sequence** rather than randomly across available forms?
3. Why did one behavior (taking paper) remain **perfectly invariant** while the adjacent behavior (making a mark) departed entirely from its stated rule?

These three anomalies have a single structural explanation. Every theorem in this framework is a consequence of that explanation. The explanation is not metaphorical. It is geometric.

## Part I — What Kind of System This Is

### 1.1 The Failure of Equilibrium Models

Any attempt to explain the park sheet using equilibrium statistical mechanics fails immediately on first contact with the data.

An equilibrium account would predict that participants, receiving the same instruction, would produce a distribution of marks centered on some modal interpretation of "a line" — variance around a mean, deviations averaging out, the collective output converging toward a stationary distribution as the session progressed. More participants would mean more convergence to the mean, not more deviation from it. The system would be **ergodic**: path-independent, history-forgetting, convergent.

The data show the structural opposite on every count.

Complexity does not converge. It escalates monotonically. The session-final distribution is the farthest from any mean that the session ever reaches. History is not forgotten — it is permanently encoded in the accumulated surface, and every subsequent mark is conditioned on that accumulated history. The path matters completely: a different sequence of agents, starting from a different initial mark, would produce a different trajectory through the space of possible outputs, and the two trajectories would diverge exponentially with each subsequent participant.

The equilibrium account is not approximately right. It predicts the wrong sign on every observable.

### 1.2 The System as a Dissipative Expressive Structure

The park sheet is an **open system**. Agents continuously enter from the environment (the park, the city, the afternoon), import cognitive energy in the form of attention, intention, and identity-expression drive, and leave behind an irreversible structural deposit: a committed mark. The sheet is not isolated. It breathes. Each new participant is an energy input; each committed mark is a structural deposit that permanently alters the landscape for everyone who follows.

Open systems driven sufficiently far from equilibrium do not converge. Under the right conditions — specifically, when driven beyond a critical threshold by sustained energy input and governed by nonlinear feedback — they **self-organize**. The system does not move toward disorder. It bifurcates. It leaps discontinuously from one organized state to a new, more complex organized state. The order it produces is not imposed from outside. It arises from and is sustained by the irreversible energy flow. This is the class of phenomena Prigogine named *dissipative structures*: order that emerges precisely because of irreversibility, not despite it.

The park sheet is a dissipative structure in expressive space. The four conditions that define a dissipative structure are all met:

**Openness.** The system exchanges cognitive energy with its environment at every moment a new participant approaches.

**Far-from-equilibrium driving.** The distinctiveness pressure — the pressure on each agent to produce a mark perceptually distinct from all prior marks — increases monotonically as the session progresses. The system is driven ever further from any stationary distribution.

**Nonlinear feedback.** Each mark reduces the available distinctiveness in its neighborhood of the expressive space, increasing the driving force on subsequent agents to travel further. The feedback is autocatalytic: marks generate more-complex marks.

**Irreversibility.** No mark can be undone. The system evolves in one direction only. Time is not reversible in this dynamics. This is not a flaw in the medium — it is, as Prigogine showed, the constructive mechanism.

The order produced by the park sheet — the sequential emergence of increasingly complex representational forms, transitioning across categorical registers in a specific lawful order — is the expressive analog of the spatial patterns produced by dissipative chemical systems. It is order from irreversibility.

### 1.3 The Arrow of Time in the Expressive Domain

In classical mechanics, time is reversible. In thermodynamics, the second law introduces an arrow: entropy increases. In the park sheet, the arrow of time is even stronger than the second law: not only does informational entropy increase monotonically, but the *specific path* of increase is unrepeatable. Each session is a unique trajectory through the expressive phase space. There is no return path and no repeat path.

Prigogine wrote that bifurcation introduces history into physics. The park sheet introduces biography into art: the collective artifact is not a description of a cultural moment but the irreversible history of a particular sequence of agents encountering a particular surface at a particular time. It could not have been otherwise given its initial conditions and its sequence of agents, and it could never be reproduced exactly.

This is the first and most fundamental theorem of the ODES framework: **the system has a permanent, path-dependent arrow of time**. Everything else follows from this.

## Part II — The Expressive Field: A Riemannian Manifold

### 2.1 Definition

The **Expressive Field** E is the space of all possible marks on the shared surface. It is a Riemannian manifold parameterized by six independent coordinates:

| Parameter | Symbol | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Topology (register) | τ | The representational mode: line · curve · loop · closed area · figure · glyph · text |
| Scale | λ | Spatial extent, length, area covered |
| Orientation | θ | Angle relative to surface axes |
| Stroke weight | w | Thickness, density, pressure |
| Chromatic position | c | Hue, saturation, luminance value |
| Surface location | (x, y) | Position on the shared surface |

A mark is a point q = (τ, λ, θ, w, c, x, y) ∈ E. The space of all possible marks is the full manifold E.

### 2.2 The Perceptual Metric

E is not Euclidean. The correct metric on E is not the abstract coordinate difference between two marks but the **perceptual distance** — how different the marks appear to the visual system of a human observer. This metric is Riemannian: it is locally Euclidean but globally curved, and the curvature encodes the non-uniformities of human visual sensitivity.

Several such non-uniformities are well-documented:

**Cardinal orientation bias.** The visual system is disproportionately sensitive to deviations from vertical and horizontal. A 5° rotation near vertical is more salient — perceptually more distinct — than the same rotation near 45°. The metric on the θ-axis is not flat; it has wells at θ = 0° and θ = 90°.

**Scale compression.** Small marks near the limit of visual resolution are perceptually closer together than large marks spanning the visual field. The metric on the λ-axis is logarithmic at large scales.

**Chromatic non-uniformity.** Perceptual color space is not linear in CIE coordinates. The metric on the c-axis reflects the empirically measured non-uniformities of human color discrimination (larger perceptual distances between colors in the blue-green region than in yellow-red).

**Topological discontinuity.** Most critically: the τ-axis is not continuous. It is a discrete set of representational categories with genuine topological barriers between them. You cannot smoothly vary from "line" to "filled area" — these are categorically distinct representational modes separated by a discontinuity that has no continuous path through.

Formally: for all parameters except τ, the metric tensor g_E(q) is smooth and positive-definite. For τ, the distance function between adjacent registers d(τ_i, τ_{i+1}) has a topological gap — a finite minimum cost that cannot be reduced by any intermediate path.

### 2.3 The Topology Register as the Critical Parameter

The register τ is the single most important parameter in E. It alone among the six parameters is **topologically discontinuous**: it does not admit smooth interpolation between its values. This is what produces the phase transitions observed in the data.

All other parameters (scale, orientation, weight, color, position) can be varied continuously. An agent can produce a mark that is slightly larger, slightly more tilted, slightly thicker, or slightly redder than any prior mark, and this produces a continuous trajectory through E. The register parameter does not admit this. There is no continuous path from "line" to "filled organic shape." The topological gap must be crossed as a discrete jump.

This discontinuity has a measurable cost. Crossing from one register to an adjacent register requires:
- Higher cognitive commitment (the agent must decide to do something categorically different)
- Higher expressive bandwidth (executing the new register requires more deliberate action than staying in the familiar one)
- Higher social exposure cost (in public settings, departing more dramatically from the stated instruction carries social risk)

This crossing cost C(τ_i → τ_{i+1}) is finite and positive for every register transition. It varies across transitions — crossing from "lines" to "curves" is cheaper than crossing from "figurative marks" to "propositional text" — but no transition is free.

The crossing costs define a **ridge structure** on the register axis: ridges of finite height separating each register from its neighbors, with the ridges growing higher as the registers grow more topologically distant from the initial instruction.

## Part III — The Pressure Landscape: Potential Energy Over E

### 3.1 Definition

At any moment t during the session, the accumulated state of the sheet S_t — the set of all marks made by participants 1 through t — defines a **Pressure Landscape** over E. This is a potential field:

```
P(q | S_t)  ∝  D_g(q, S_t)
```

where D_g(q, S_t) is the geodesic distance from the proposed mark q to the nearest existing mark in S_t, measured under the Riemannian metric g_E of E.

Plain language: **high pressure means the region is unoccupied and available. Low pressure means the region is already taken.** Agents driven by identity-expression needs are attracted toward high-pressure regions — toward territory in E not yet claimed by prior participants.

### 3.2 The Four Structural Laws of the Pressure Landscape

**Law 1: The Ratchet.** Every committed mark permanently lowers the pressure P in its perceptual neighborhood. The pressure at any point in E can only decrease or remain constant — it cannot increase except by reaching unexplored territory. There is no restoring force. The Pressure Landscape evolves monotonically in the direction of increasing occupation. This is the direct expressive analog of the second law of thermodynamics.

Formally: for all q ∈ E and all t' > t: P(q | S_{t'}) ≤ P(q | S_t). The ratchet is strict.

**Law 2: Geometric Escalation.** As occupied regions expand, maintaining equivalent distinctiveness — reaching territory with equivalent pressure — requires traveling further in E. The geodesic distance that was sufficient for distinctiveness at session-time t₃ is insufficient at session-time t₁₃. This is not a cultural choice or a creative ambition. It is arithmetic: a space is filling, and reaching the same distance from the nearest occupied point requires going further as the space fills. Complexity escalation is the inevitable geometry of a filling Riemannian manifold.

**Law 3: Ridge Structure.** The register transitions τ_i → τ_{i+1} are topological ridges in the Pressure Landscape — barriers of finite height that are costly to cross. An agent crosses a ridge only when: P_within ≤ C_ridge, i.e., when the pressure available within the current register falls below the cost of crossing to the adjacent register. Below this threshold, the gradient of available pressure points across the ridge rather than along the current register. The agent crosses not in defiance of the geometry but in obedience to it.

**Law 4: Spatial Autocorrelation.** The Pressure Landscape is not uniform across the physical surface. Agents condition their field-reading on locally visible prior marks — they do not scan the entire sheet before every mark. This produces spatial clustering: adjacent marks are made by agents who saw similar prior marks and responded to them. The spatial autocorrelation of mark complexity is a measurable, structural property of the accumulated sheet, not a random pattern.

### 3.3 The Entropy Production Rate

Define the **informational entropy** of the accumulated state S_t as the Shannon entropy of the mark distribution across E:

```
H(S_t)  =  −Σ_q  p(q | S_t) · log p(q | S_t)
```

where p(q | S_t) is the empirical density of marks in the neighborhood of q. This quantity measures how much of E has been explored and how uniformly it has been covered.

H(S_t) has three critical properties:

**Monotonic increase.** Each new mark occupies previously unoccupied territory in E (driven by distinctiveness pressure). Every participant adds information — expands the covered region of E. H(S_t) is non-decreasing for all t. It cannot decrease: marks cannot be removed.

**Accelerated increase at register transitions.** When the session crosses a register boundary — when the first filled organic shape appears, when the first figurative mark appears, when propositional text appears — H(S_t) jumps discontinuously. A new topological region of E is entered, expanding the covered space qualitatively rather than quantitatively. These are the **bifurcation events**: moments at which the prior stable organized state (the current register) becomes insufficient and the system leaps to a new, higher-complexity organized state. They are the expressive analog of Hopf bifurcations in chemical dissipative systems.

**Path dependence.** H(S_t) encodes the full history of the session's trajectory through E. Two sessions with different initial conditions will produce different entropy trajectories. The sessions' trajectories will diverge exponentially with each subsequent participant, and the divergence is irreversible.

The **entropy production rate** σ_irrev(t) = dH/dt measures the rate at which the session is expanding into new territory in E. This rate is not uniform. It is:
- Moderate in the interior of a register (marks are varied but staying in the same topological category)
- Elevated at register transitions (new topological category opens, large new region of E becomes accessible)
- Maximal when propositional text appears (the entire linguistic register of E — vast and previously unexplored — is entered for the first time)

## Part IV — The Attention Allocation Theorem

### 4.1 The Two-Signal Problem

Every participant faced two simultaneous signals before marking:

**Signal A** (the instruction): *Draw a line.* Fixed. Unambiguous. Identical for every agent throughout the entire session.

**Signal B** (the accumulated surface): The growing, increasingly complex collection of all prior marks. Not fixed. Not unambiguous. Unique at every moment t.

Signal A costs near-zero attention to process: it is short, verbal, explicit, and unambiguous. Signal B costs attention proportional to the complexity of the accumulated state S_t: a sparse sheet is cheap to read; a dense, complex sheet demands sustained visual attention to parse, locate unoccupied territory, and assess distinctiveness.

### 4.2 The Theorem

**Theorem [ODES-T1]** *(Attention Reallocation under Growing Complexity).* Let κ be an agent's finite attention bandwidth. Let I_A be the information load of the instruction signal (constant) and I_B(t) be the information load of the accumulated state S_t (strictly increasing). Then the attention allocated to Signal A decreases monotonically with t:

```
α_A(t)  =  κ · I_A / (I_A + I_B(t))

dα_A/dt  <  0    (strictly, since I_B(t) is strictly increasing)
```

At session onset (t small, S_t sparse): I_B ≈ 0, α_A ≈ 1. The agent reads the instruction, which dominates. They draw a line.

At late session (t large, S_t complex): I_B >> I_A, α_A → 0. The accumulated surface dominates. The instruction is not forgotten — it has been outcompeted for cognitive resources by the growing visual complexity of the sheet itself.

This theorem explains **rule drift without invoking disobedience or creativity**. Late-session participants are not being disobedient. They are allocating their finite cognitive resources to the signal that is most demanding and most informative for the task of distinctiveness. The instruction is a fixed, low-information signal. The sheet is a variable, high-information signal. Rational attention allocation produces progressive abandonment of the instruction.

### 4.3 The Instruction Paradox

Theorem ODES-T1 generates a paradox that is a prediction, not an anomaly: **the richer the instruction, the faster it is abandoned**. A more elaborate instruction carries a higher initial I_A — it demands more attention at the start — but this makes it even faster to be outcompeted by I_B(t) once the sheet begins to fill. The instruction is not neutral. It is a finite resource that competes with an expanding one.

The only instruction that could survive indefinitely is one that scales with I_B(t) — one that updates to respond to the accumulated state. A fixed instruction cannot compete with a growing field.

## Part V — Agent Trajectory Mechanics

### 5.1 The Phase Space

Each agent's state is not just a position in E but a position together with a direction and magnitude of expressive drive. The full state space is the **cotangent bundle** T*E: each agent at time t occupies a point q ∈ E (where they mark) together with a cotangent vector p ∈ T*_q E (the direction and intensity of their distinctiveness drive at that point).

The agent's trajectory through E is a curve in this cotangent bundle, governed by a Hamiltonian H(q, p) on T*E that encodes the balance between distinctiveness drive (the pressure landscape P) and the costs of traversal (the Riemannian metric g_E and the crossing costs C_ridge).

### 5.2 The Four Acts of Agent Navigation

Every participant navigates the Pressure Landscape through four sequential acts. These are not arbitrary behavioral stages. They are the necessary structure of any agent with finite cognitive resources navigating a curved potential field under a growing information load.

**Act I — Field Reading.**
The agent scans the accumulated surface, implicitly constructing a local map of the Pressure Landscape: which regions of E are saturated, where the pressure gradient points, what territory remains open. Map precision scales with available attention bandwidth κ_agent. A glance produces a coarse map — the agent sees only the most salient occupied regions and moves toward the most obvious unoccupied territory. Sustained examination produces a fine map — the agent identifies subtle gaps, perceptually underexplored regions invisible to a casual scan.

Coarse maps produce gradient-following along the steepest locally visible path: the agent goes to the most obvious available place in E. Fine maps permit identification of unpopulated pockets — the agent reaches a region that is genuinely distinct because they looked longer. This is why the mandala and the text required more time. They required finer maps.

**Act II — Gradient Following.**
The agent moves through E along the geodesic of the pressure-weighted metric — the path of minimum resistance toward maximum distinctiveness from all prior marks. Their starting position in this movement is shaped by personal history (the marks they know how to make) and cultural toolkit (the range of representational forms they carry). But the *direction* of movement is governed by the Pressure Landscape, not by personal preference in isolation.

This is not origination of something from nothing. It is navigation of a field. Gradient following in E is the expressive analog of gradient descent in an optimization landscape: the agent moves in the direction of steepest available distinctiveness, not in the direction they arrived planning to go.

**Act III — Natural Stopping.**
The agent stops when one of three conditions is met:

- **(a) Distinctiveness achieved:** The proposed mark has sufficient geodesic distance from all prior marks in g_E. The agent has reached territory in E that is genuinely their own.
- **(b) Bandwidth exhausted:** The agent has committed the cognitive resources they brought. Further navigation requires more attention than is available.
- **(c) Gradient reversal:** Further movement in the current direction would begin to reduce distinctiveness — the gradient of available pressure reverses, and the agent is approaching territory already occupied from the other side.

Condition (c) explains why the mandala did not fill the entire sheet. The agent followed the pressure gradient until further elaboration would have started reducing perceptual distinctiveness from existing marks — until elaboration began to loop back toward occupied territory rather than away from it. Then they stopped. This is the natural stopping condition of gradient-following in a bounded potential field.

**Act IV — Irreversible Commitment.**
The mark is drawn. Ink touches paper. This single act permanently alters the Pressure Landscape for every subsequent participant. There is no undo. The informational entropy H(S_t) increases by the amount of new territory occupied. The system's trajectory through E is extended by one irreversible step.

Irreversibility is not a limitation of the medium. It is the **generative mechanism** of the entire framework. Without irreversibility, there is no ratchet. Without a ratchet, there is no monotonic pressure escalation. Without escalation, there are no register crossings. Without register crossings, there is no ordered complexity sequence. The art is a consequence of the permanence of the marks.

## Part VI — Register Topology: Phase Transitions in the Expressive Field

### 6.1 The Register Sequence as a Topological Necessity

As the session progresses and the within-register pressure falls below the crossing cost C_ridge, agents cross into adjacent registers. This is **Register Escape**: not rebellion, not creativity in the sense of autonomous origination, but the gradient pointing across the topological ridge because within-register territory is exhausted.

The register escape sequence observed in the photograph:

```
τ₀: Vertical strokes
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₁: Diagonal strokes → Varied angle strokes
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₂: Cursive loops → S-curves
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₃: Filled organic shape (2-dimensional area)
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₄: Figurative marks (lightning bolt, smiley face)
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₅: Complex figurative (mandala, spiral)
    ↓ [within-register saturation]
τ₆: Propositional text
```

This sequence is not arbitrary. It follows the topological structure of E: the registers are ordered by increasing topological complexity (the number of degrees of freedom their instantiation requires), increasing cognitive cost (the bandwidth demanded to produce them), and increasing informational density (the Shannon entropy of an individual mark in that register). The sequence from τ₀ to τ₆ is the sequence of increasing topological dimension in the representational space.

This is a formal prediction: **alternative orderings of this sequence should be impossible or very rare** in sessions with equivalent initial conditions. The sequence is determined by the topology of E, not by the cultural preferences of participants.

### 6.2 The Propositional Text as the Terminal Bifurcation

The written sentence — *Art is inextricable from ideas* — is the terminal and most dramatic register escape. By the time that agent arrived, the visual registers τ₀ through τ₅ were substantially occupied. Every topological category of visual mark was represented. The one region of E with near-zero prior occupancy was **propositional linguistic space**: the register of structured natural language argument.

No prior participant had placed a connected propositional claim on the sheet. The pressure in the linguistic register was absolute — it was the most distant unoccupied region of E accessible to a human agent. An agent with sufficient bandwidth, intellectual engagement with the prompt, and willingness to pay the highest crossing cost in the session followed the gradient all the way to the edge of the manifold and placed a counter-proposition to the instruction's implicit claim.

*Art is inextricable from ideas* directly contradicts the poster's claim *ART ≠ IDEAS*. This is not coincidence. It is the geometry of the Pressure Landscape: the most occupied region of E at the end of the session was the visual-expressive register established by the poster. The most available territory was the propositional-argumentative register — the one in which the poster made a claim but no participant had yet responded in kind.

The text is the highest-entropy event in the dataset: the most informationally dense, the most topologically distant from the initial instruction, and the strongest empirical confirmation of the framework's predictions. An agent followed the pressure gradient across the largest topological ridge in the session and placed the most distinct mark possible: a direct logical negation of the founding condition.

### 6.3 Phase Transition Geometry

Each register escape is a bifurcation in the dynamical sense. The prior organized state — the current register, with its coherent cluster of marks and its diminishing available pressure — becomes unstable. The system cannot remain in the prior state: the within-register pressure has fallen below the crossing cost. A new organized state — the next register, with its fresh territory and high available pressure — becomes accessible. The system leaps discontinuously to the new state.

This is structurally identical to the Hopf bifurcation in dissipative chemical systems: a steady state loses stability at a critical parameter value, and the system undergoes a discontinuous transition to a new, more complex organized state. In chemical systems, the bifurcation parameter is the distance from equilibrium. In the expressive system, the bifurcation parameter is the within-register pressure P_within relative to the crossing cost C_ridge.

Define the **Register Bifurcation Condition**:

```
P_within(t)  ≤  C(τ_current → τ_next)
```

This condition, when crossed, produces a register escape with probability approaching 1 for agents with sufficient expressive bandwidth. The sequence of register escapes in the session is the sequence of these bifurcations.

## Part VII — The Invariant: A Bandwidth Budget Theorem

### 7.1 The Two Behaviors

The park session produced one perfect behavioral invariant: everyone took a piece of paper. Not most — everyone. In a session where the expressive behavior departed so completely from its instruction that it ended with propositional philosophical argument, the transactional behavior of paper-taking never deviated at all.

This is not a coincidence. It is a theorem.

**Theorem [ODES-T2]** *(The Invariant Theorem).* Let B_A be a behavior that requires near-zero cognitive bandwidth to execute (B_A is motoric, unambiguous, and socially visible as a compliance signal). Let B_B be a behavior that competes for the same finite bandwidth as the attention-demanding task of Pressure Landscape navigation. Then:

As I_B(t) grows with session time t:
- B_A compliance → 1 (invariant)
- B_B rule-following → 0 (total drift)

**Proof sketch:** Paper-taking satisfies the bandwidth conditions for B_A: it is motoric (pick up paper), unambiguous (the paper is there, the motion is clear), and socially visible (other people can see whether you took paper, providing instant compliance signaling). Its information cost I_{B_A} ≈ 0. Therefore α_{B_A}(t) = κ · I_{B_A} / (I_{B_A} + I_B(t)) ≈ κ · 0 / I_B(t) = 0... wait, the relevant statement is that compliance is protected from competition because the *cost* of taking paper is near zero regardless of I_B(t). No attention is diverted from I_B processing to B_A execution. They do not compete.

Mark-making fails this condition: it competes directly for the same cognitive bandwidth as Pressure Landscape navigation. The more attention the landscape demands, the less is available for instruction-following. B_B rule-following falls monotonically as I_B(t) grows.

The invariant and the drift are not two separate phenomena. They are one phenomenon — finite attention allocation — seen from two sides.

### 7.2 Prediction from the Invariant Theorem

The invariant behavior can be identified a priori by applying two tests: (1) does it require near-zero cognitive bandwidth? (2) does it carry social compliance visibility? Any instruction element that passes both tests will be invariant regardless of how far the adjacent expressive behaviors drift. Any element that fails either test is subject to drift proportional to I_B(t).

This is a testable prediction with implications beyond art: in any setting where sequential agents must comply with a low-bandwidth requirement while making a high-bandwidth expressive decision, the low-bandwidth compliance will be perfect and the high-bandwidth expression will drift completely. The drift is not evidence of non-compliance — it is evidence of correct attention allocation.

## Part VIII — Anti-Ergodic Accumulation

### 8.1 The Definition

A system is **ergodic** if, given sufficient time, it explores its state space uniformly and settles into a stationary distribution. Ergodic systems are path-independent, history-forgetting, and convergent. Their time averages equal their ensemble averages. Deviations from the mean are temporary and average out.

A system is **anti-ergodic** if it structurally cannot satisfy any of these conditions. The park sheet is anti-ergodic in a strong and precise sense.

**Theorem [ODES-T3]** *(Anti-Ergodic Accumulation).* Any system satisfying the four ODES conditions — (1) agents have identity-expression drives, (2) the surface is publicly observable, (3) outputs are irreversible, (4) no coordination payoff exists for similarity — cannot converge to any stationary distribution. The system's trajectory through E diverges without bound from any fixed distribution, and the rate of divergence is bounded below by the entropy production rate σ_irrev(t).

**Proof of non-convergence:** Convergence to a stationary distribution π on E would require that the probability of occupying any region R ⊂ E stabilize over time. This requires that agents produce marks in R at a stable rate. But the Ratchet Law (Law 1) guarantees that each mark in R permanently reduces the attractiveness of R for subsequent agents: the pressure P(q | S_t) for q ∈ R decreases monotonically. No stable rate of production in R is possible — the rate of production in any bounded region of E must fall to zero as the region becomes occupied. The distribution over marks cannot stabilize; it must shift toward unoccupied territory permanently.

**Proof that detailed balance fails:** Standard ergodic systems satisfy detailed balance: π(A)T(A→B) = π(B)T(B→A) — the probability flux from state A to state B equals the flux in reverse. In the park system, the irreversibility of committed marks makes the reverse transition impossible: the mark made by agent 3 cannot be unmade by agent 7. T(B→A) = 0 for all A ≠ B. Detailed balance fails everywhere. The system is not merely non-ergodic; it violates the preconditions of ergodicity at the most fundamental level.

### 8.2 Path Dependence as a Structural Property

The irreversibility that makes the system anti-ergodic also makes it **maximally path-dependent**. The accumulated state S_t at time t is the complete, indelible history of the session from t=0 to t. Every trajectory decision made by every prior agent is permanently encoded in the Pressure Landscape that the current agent navigates. No summary statistic of S_t — no mean, no variance, no distribution over mark types — contains the same information as S_t itself. The full history is the full state.

This has a strong empirical corollary: **two sessions with identical instructions and equivalent participant populations, seeded with different initial marks, will produce statistically distinguishable trajectories with probability approaching 1 as the number of participants grows**. The initial seed (the first mark, the initial Pressure Landscape set by the poster) determines the curvature of the entire subsequent trajectory. Small differences in initial conditions compound exponentially.

## Part IX — The Poster as Prior: The Founder Effect

### 9.1 The Prompt Is Always Already in the Field

The experiment notes describe the instruction as "draw a line." But the participants did not encounter the instruction in a vacuum. They encountered it on a poster rendered in **multiple colors, large irregular hand-drawn letterforms, layered stylized strokes with evident aesthetic care**. The ≠ symbol itself was expressive. The poster was art.

Every participant encountered this visual object before approaching the mark sheet. It instantiated an initial Pressure Landscape S_0 before the first mark was drawn. The tally strokes of the earliest participants are not simple relative to zero complexity — they are simple relative to the poster's complexity. The first agents were already navigating a field shaped by a prior aesthetic commitment.

This means the experiment did not begin when the first participant marked. It began when the poster was made.

### 9.2 The Founder Effect

Define the **Founder Effect** as the initial curvature of the Pressure Landscape set by the prompt before any participant marks. This curvature is the initial condition of the system. It determines:

- Which regions of E are already occupied (or perceptually depleted) at the session's start
- Which regions therefore carry the highest initial pressure
- The direction of the first participants' gradient-following
- Consequently, the entire subsequent bifurcation sequence

The poster establishes a pre-existing pressure well in the visual-aesthetic register. It occupies E in the direction of expressive hand-drawn visual complexity. The first participants cannot achieve distinctiveness in that direction — it is already claimed by the poster. They go to the simplest available territory: near-vertical strokes that are maximally distant from the poster's complexity while still being marks.

In other words: a richer, more complex prompt produces earlier register escapes because it preemptively occupies more of E before the session begins. The poster is not a neutral context. It is the founding agent whose mark persists throughout the entire session — the one agent who never re-encounters the field they left.

## Part X — The ODES Equations

### 10.1 The State Equation

The state of the system at time t is fully specified by S_t — the set of all marks made through time t. The evolution equation is:

```
S_{t+1}  =  S_t  ∪  {q*(t+1)}

q*(t+1)  =  argmax_{q ∈ E\S_t}  P(q | S_t)    subject to  ||q||_κ ≤ κ_{t+1}
```

where P(q | S_t) = D_g(q, S_t) is the pressure at q given accumulated state S_t, and ||q||_κ is the cognitive cost of producing mark q, bounded by agent t+1's attention bandwidth κ_{t+1}.

In words: each agent marks the point in E with the highest available distinctiveness pressure that they can reach within their attention budget. This is the ODES trajectory equation.

### 10.2 The Entropy Production Equation

The informational entropy production at each step is:

```
ΔH(t)  =  H(S_{t+1}) − H(S_t)  =  D_g(q*(t+1), S_t)  ·  ρ(q*(t+1))
```

where ρ(q) is the local density of unexplored territory in the neighborhood of q in E. At register transitions, ρ jumps discontinuously — a new topological region opens, vastly expanding the available unexplored territory. These are the entropy production peaks at bifurcation events.

### 10.3 The Register Escape Equation

Register escape from τ_current to τ_next occurs when:

```
P_max(τ_current | S_t)  ≤  C(τ_current → τ_next)
```

where P_max(τ_current | S_t) is the maximum available pressure within the current register, and C(τ_current → τ_next) is the crossing cost of the topological ridge between them. At this moment, the gradient points across the ridge. The agent with sufficient bandwidth crosses.

## Part XII — Connections to Existing Work

These are structural parallels, not citations deployed for authority. Each confirms a component of the ODES mechanism or reveals where the framework differs from prior accounts.

### Dissipative Structures (Prigogine 1977)

Open systems far from thermodynamic equilibrium self-organize into ordered states sustained by irreversible energy flow, undergoing discontinuous bifurcations as the system is driven beyond successive instability thresholds. The park sheet is precisely such a system in the expressive domain: open (agents enter and exit), far from equilibrium (no convergence toward a stationary mark distribution), and producing order (coherent register clusters, lawful escalation sequence) through irreversible entropy production. Each register escape is a bifurcation: the prior organized state (current register) becomes unstable and the system leaps to a new organized state (next register). The bifurcation history is the history of the session.

The critical difference from chemical dissipative structures: in chemical systems, the driving parameter (temperature, reactant concentration) can be controlled externally. In the expressive system, the driving parameter (within-register pressure) is generated endogenously by the agents' own prior marks. The system drives itself toward its bifurcation points.

### Iterated Learning (Kirby, Cornish & Smith 2008)

Artificial languages transmitted across participant chains became more structured through compression pressure: agents simplified and regularized to ease transmission. The ODES framework is the structural inverse — same iterated transmission architecture, **divergence pressure** instead of compression pressure. The direction of the gradient (toward learnability vs. toward distinctiveness) fully determines whether the system converges or diverges. Both results follow from the same underlying mechanism — agents modify outputs relative to prior outputs — applied to opposite gradient fields.

### Cumulative Cultural Ratchet (Caldwell & Millen 2008)

Paper plane designs improved across chains without explicit instruction through irreversible ratcheting. Each generation's improvement was permanently locked in. This ratchet mechanism is identical to ODES's Ratchet Law (Law 1). The difference: their ratchet improves along a functional performance dimension; ODES escalates along a complexity-in-E dimension. Both demonstrate that irreversibility + gradient = monotonic directed change. Neither requires intent, coordination, or explicit instruction.

### Schema and Correction (Gombrich 1960)

All representation begins with a prior schema (a simplified prototype) corrected toward observation. In the ODES framework, the schema is the accumulated prior state S_t and the correction is driven by distinctiveness pressure rather than perceptual accuracy. Gombrich's mechanism predicts that each correction is relative to what already exists — which is the gradient-following property of ODES Act II. But Gombrich's correction converges (toward more accurate representation of the world); ODES's correction diverges (toward less similar representation of prior marks).

### Optimal Complexity and Berlyne's Inverted-U (Berlyne 1971)

Humans prefer an intermediate level of stimulation complexity — too simple produces under-stimulation, too complex produces overwhelm. Maximum engagement falls on the ascending edge of this inverted-U. The ODES framework predicts that each agent targets this ascending edge: their mark should be more complex than the existing marks by just enough to be distinctively stimulating. As the existing sheet grows richer, the target moves upward. Complexity escalation tracks a moving optimality target defined by the accumulated state.

### Schelling Dynamics and Minimal Rules (Schelling 1978)

Minimal local rules produce dramatic macro-level patterns with no central coordination. The ODES framework is a Schelling-type system: the local rule is minimal ("be distinct from what is already there"), and the macro pattern (escalating complexity, phase transitions, register escape sequence, perfect invariant) emerges from purely local agent behavior. No coordination is needed. No intention toward complexity is needed. The complexity is produced by the geometry.

### GRAIN (this framework family)

The GRAIN framework (Gradient Rationality and Autocorrelation Instinct in Nature) models agents navigating a patchy fitness landscape under spatial autocorrelation, driven by foraging gradient-following toward high-yield patches. ODES models agents navigating a distinctiveness pressure landscape, driven by distinctiveness gradient-following toward unoccupied territory in E. Both are gradient-following systems on Riemannian manifolds. Both produce monotonic directed behavior from local optimization rules. The key difference is the direction of the gradient and the ratchet mechanism:

- GRAIN: gradient toward high fitness; landscape is externally given and partially persistent
- ODES: gradient toward high distinctiveness; landscape is endogenously generated and permanently ratcheting

The Attention Allocation Theorem (ODES-T1) has a GRAIN analog: the GRAIN spectral gap governs how much of an agent's attention is productively allocated to tracking the first eigenmode of the fitness landscape. Both frameworks formalize the competition for finite cognitive bandwidth between a fixed instruction and a growing information source.

## Part XIII — Where the Framework Is Incomplete

**Individual bandwidth is a free parameter.** ODES predicts that agents with higher attention bandwidth will travel further in E and cross register ridges more readily. It does not derive the distribution of bandwidth across agents from first principles. Why the mandala-maker and the text-writer had the bandwidth required for their register crossings — rather than just predicting that *some* agent eventually would — is outside the framework's current scope.

**Crossing costs are unspecified.** The framework predicts register escapes when within-register pressure falls below the crossing cost C_ridge. It does not yet specify how C_ridge is determined. Is it purely cognitive (the topological distance in E)? Does it include social exposure cost (public visibility of norm departure)? Both factors are plausible. A full specification would allow quantitative prediction of which register a session of N participants reaches, not just the qualitative sequence.

**N was not recorded.** The participant count in the founding experiment is unknown. The framework's predictions about when register escapes occur are functions of t (participant number). Without the actual sequence, these predictions cannot be fully tested against the existing data. Any replication must record this.

**The metric g_E is not yet empirically measured.** The framework requires the Riemannian metric of E to be specified by human perceptual sensitivity across all six parameters. This is measurable using established psychophysical methods (discrimination tasks across the parameter axes), but has not yet been done for the full manifold.

**One session, one city, one afternoon.** The framework claims generalizability across contexts. That claim requires replication in different physical settings, different cultural contexts, different instruction regimes, and different prompt aesthetics before it can be stated with confidence beyond a hypothesis.

## Part XIV — Predictions for Replication

If the framework is correct, the following should hold in controlled replications:

**1. Register sequence is topologically necessary.**
The ordering τ₀ → ... → τ₆ should replicate across independent sessions. Departures should be traceable to outlier-bandwidth agents, unusual initial conditions, or different poster aesthetics — not random variation.

**2. Spatial clustering by register is measurable.**
The spatial autocorrelation coefficient ρ of mark complexity across the surface should be significantly positive. Marks should be locally clustered by register. Null model: uniform spatial distribution.

**3. The invariant holds under extreme expressive drift.**
Paper-taking compliance should remain near 100% even when the expressive behavior includes propositional philosophical argument. Test: count paper-taking events versus mark register at late-session marks.

**4. Founder effect shifts bifurcation timing.**
Two parallel sessions: one with the aesthetically complex poster (H₀ > 0), one with a minimal text-only instruction (H₀ ≈ 0). The complex-poster session should produce earlier first register escape and reach higher final register depth at equivalent N.

**5. Path dependence produces divergent trajectories.**
Seed two sessions with different experimenter-planted initial marks. Run with equivalent participant pools. The trajectories through E should diverge, and divergence should increase with each subsequent participant. Measure divergence by comparing H(S_t^A, S_t^B) — the cross-entropy between the two sessions' accumulated states.

**6. Entropy production accelerates at bifurcations.**
If marks are coded by register and perceptual distance from prior marks, H(S_t) should show discontinuous positive jumps at register escape events. The jump magnitude should increase with the register transition index (later escapes produce larger entropy jumps, because higher registers occupy more volume in E).

**7. The instruction paradox is observable.**
A richer, more elaborate initial instruction (higher I_A) should produce faster attention reallocation away from the instruction as the session progresses. Late-session marks in the elaborate-instruction condition should diverge further from the instruction than in the minimal-instruction condition, controlling for session length.
